## Review

Scope: red-team of RESEARCH.md, PLAN.md, RESULTS.md against the actual implementation (model.py, train.py) and run logs. Read-only; no files modified.

### Correct (worth stating)

- The core training mechanism is implemented as claimed: soft-CE predictor loss backprops into the backbone in joint mode (model.py:159, train.py:78-81), the target is realized top-k indices (non-differentiable, no target leakage), and chance-level sanity passed (0.25 hit@k for untrained heads in run_tierB_base.log).
- Dose-response is monotone and internally consistent across the λ ablation (run_lambda.log), and the logs support the "posthoc roughly plateaued" claim (hit@k 0.842@500 → 0.849@2000, run_tierB_posthoc.log).
- Baseline and joint runs see identical data order (deterministic stream, same seed), so within-experiment A/B data is matched — up to the eval-consumption drift below.

### Strongest attacks (numbered, severity + fix)

**1. The "zero quality cost" claim is two orders of magnitude below the eval noise floor — SEVERITY: CRITICAL.**
Evidence: two runs of the *identical* baseline (same seed, same architecture, λ=0) produce final "val lm" of 5.942 (run_tierB_base.log, Exp 1) and 6.285 (run_h124_rest.log, Exp 2) — a 0.343-nat spread. Within a single run, consecutive evals swing 5.78 → 6.28 (run_h124_rest.log steps 4500→6000). The quality claims in RESULTS.md rest on deltas of 0.005 nats (5.937 vs 5.942) and 0.014 nats ("free up to λ=0.3", 6.299 vs 6.285). These are unmeasurable with a 5-batch eval (`evaluate(model, batch_fn, steps=5)`, train.py:37) on a noisy stream. Similarly the +2.5pt hit@k effects are comparable to observed eval-to-eval hit@k jitter (joint h4: 0.802@2000/4500 → 0.760@6000, run_h124.log — a 4.2pt swing on an unchanged checkpoint trajectory).
Fix: fixed held-out eval set (≥50 batches, disjoint from the training stream), ≥3 seeds, report mean ± 95% CI, and paired per-chunk comparisons. Until then, "zero cost" and "+2.5pt" are not established.

**2. The central causal claim — "the backbone became more predictable" — is never isolated from predictor co-adaptation — SEVERITY: CRITICAL.**
RESULTS.md Exp 3 asserts "everything above λ=0.03 is genuine training-induced predictability." But hit@k is always measured with the *co-trained* predictor. The gain could live in the predictor (6000 steps of co-adaptation to a moving backbone) rather than in the backbone's intrinsic predictability. This matters doubly because the deployment thesis (Colibri trains its own predictor against your model) requires the predictability to be a property of the *model*.
Fix (one cheap run, no new training of backbones): load ckpt_lam0.3_joint.pt in `--mode posthoc` (train.py already supports this via `--ckpt`), train a *fresh* predictor on the frozen joint backbone, compare its hit@k to the co-trained predictor's 0.900/0.877/0.800. If equal → claim holds and is deployable. If lower → the effect is co-adaptation, not predictability, and the paper's framing collapses. Also run the mirror control: posthoc predictor on the *baseline* backbone for a matched 6000 steps to close the 2000-vs-6000 steps gap.

**3. Degenerate predictability is not ruled out: no routing entropy, utilization, or cross-layer routing-MI measurements — SEVERITY: HIGH.**
The loss (model.py:159) rewards the predictor matching realized routing; the backbone can satisfy this by making routing *simpler*, not more informed — e.g., router decisions that copy the previous layer (inflating h=2/h=4 trivially), collapse onto a static token→expert map, or reduced specialization. The load-balancing loss (model.py:104-106) only guards marginal uniformity, which is compatible with all of these degenerate solutions. λ=1.0 paying +0.24 nats while gaining +11pts is exactly the signature you'd expect from routing distortion, and nothing measured distinguishes "distortion" from "predictability" at λ=0.1–0.3.
Fix: log per-layer router entropy, expert utilization histogram, and mutual information between adjacent layers' routing (base vs joint). Add a "shuffle-control": hit@k of predicting layer l+h routing from layer l when the joint model's routing is artificially decorrelated — or simpler, check whether joint h=4 gain is explained by routing persistence (P(same experts at l and l+1)).

**4. hit@k is not the prefetch objective; no cache-budget, cost-weighted, or waste-penalized metric exists — SEVERITY: HIGH.**
The metric (model.py:163-164) is uniform per-token overlap of predicted top-2 with true top-2. Prefetch success is recall under a *cache budget* with a replacement policy, where (a) a miss costs one expert's bytes of latency, (b) a *wrong* prefetch wastes NVMe bandwidth — actively harmful under Colibri's batch-union reads, (c) prefetch depth k_pred is a free choice (predicting 4 to cover 2 true experts changes the economics entirely and is unexplored), and (d) the relevant baseline is not chance but LRU/hot-store cache hit rate. PLAN.md's own step 5 (hit-rate → cache simulation → tok/s) is unchecked; zero evidence currently converts +4.3pts into throughput.
Fix: build the planned cache simulator; report recall@{budget} against LRU-only and hot-store baselines, with a misprefetch-bandwidth penalty term; sweep k_pred ∈ {2,3,4}.

**5. The posthoc "SOTA control" is undermatched on three axes — SEVERITY: HIGH.**
(a) *Capacity/protocol*: the control is a single linear head with soft-CE (model.py:122-141); the cited SOTA it claims to replicate (arXiv 2511.10676) uses two linear layers + ranking-aware loss. Labeling it "SOTA control" (RESULTS.md Exp 1) overstates the strawman... or understates the baseline — either way the comparison is not against SOTA. (b) *Steps*: 2000 vs 6000 (run_tierB_posthoc.log vs run_tierB_joint.log); pred loss was still declining (1.211→1.199) at cutoff. (c) *Stream position*: the posthoc run restarts the FineWeb stream at offset 0 (train.py:21 has no skip/resume), so its final eval is at ~8.4M tokens while joint evals sit at ~24.6M — hit@k compared across different data segments, with per-chunk hit@k variance shown to be ±4pts (attack 1).
Fix: matched-capacity (2-layer, ranking loss) posthoc control, trained to convergence on the same stream segment, evaluated on the same held-out chunks as the joint model.

**6. "Val LM loss" is the training objective on training-distribution data — SEVERITY: HIGH for the quality claim.**
`fineweb_stream` uses `split="train"` (train.py:21) and `evaluate()` draws from the *same iterator* as training (train.py:37, 96). There is no held-out split, no independent corpus, no downstream task. Since joint training optimizes this exact objective, equality on it cannot detect specialization/generalization damage (the plausible cost of predictability pressure). Additionally, evals *consume* the training stream: Exp 1 (eval-every 250, 24 evals) vs Exp 2 (eval-every 500, 16 evals per log counts) skip different amounts of data, so the two "identical" baselines actually trained on different token streams after step 250 — one concrete contributor to the 5.942-vs-6.285 discrepancy, and a reproducibility hole in every cross-experiment comparison in RESULTS.md.
Fix: separate eval iterator on a disjoint split/corpus (e.g., OpenWebText or held-out FineWeb-edu shard), one lightweight downstream probe, and never draw eval batches from the training stream.

**7. Single seed, no inferential statistics — SEVERITY: MEDIUM-HIGH.**
`torch.manual_seed(0)` (train.py:72) for every run; all six table rows in Exp 3 are n=1. ROCm bf16 nondeterminism means even "same seed" isn't bit-reproducible, which cuts both ways. RESULTS.md's own note that "joint evals fluctuated mid-run... so same-step final evals are used" is an admission that the reported numbers are one noisy sample chosen after observing the noise.
Fix: 3 seeds × the fixed held-out eval from attack 1; report the λ dose-response with error bars (the monotone trend will likely survive; the "free up to 0.3" threshold will move).

**8. The end-to-end thesis is untested and has identifiable regimes where prediction loses — SEVERITY: MEDIUM-HIGH.**
Tier B experts are ~1.6MB (2×384×1024×bf16); the whole model is ~190MB — nothing is ever disk-resident, so this tier *cannot* exercise the prefetch scenario; the planned mmap/pread harness (PLAN.md infra step 5) is unbuilt. Even with perfect prediction, prefetch does not pay when: (a) the hot expert set fits in the cache budget — Colibri's learned hot-store + LRU already captures this, and prediction only wins on the *non-stationary tail*; (b) prefill — a batch's union of experts approaches the full set, making per-token prediction worthless (only single-token decode benefits); (c) the workload is compute- or bandwidth-bound rather than latency-bound (wrong prefetches then strictly hurt by consuming bandwidth); (d) horizon ≥2 on real models, where frozen-model accuracy decays sharply — the regime where the method should shine is exactly where the toy shows the *smallest* absolute headroom. And the reactive baseline is strong: Colibri's PILOT=1 gets 71.6% one-layer-ahead for free.
Fix: run the planned pread benchmark at a forced-small cache budget (e.g., 2 of 8 experts resident) comparing LRU-only, PILOT-style one-layer reactive, and joint-predictor prefetch; report tok/s and wasted-bytes, and state explicitly which regime (decode, budget < working set) the claim applies to.

**9. Scale transfer from 8-expert/8-layer/25M-token Tier B to 256-expert/75-layer production is asserted, not argued — SEVERITY: MEDIUM.**
(a) 8 experts top-2 = 25% of capacity active per token per layer with chance 0.25; GLM-5.2's 256 experts top-8 ≈ 3% with chance ~0.03 — prediction difficulty, router entropy (~log E), and cache economics are categorically different. (b) Horizon 4 of 8 layers = half the network; at 75 layers a 2-layer lead is 2.7% of depth with very different cross-layer information decay. (c) The models are ~75× undertrained vs Chinchilla (25M tokens, 94M params); undertrained routers are weakly specialized, plausibly inflating *both* the frozen baseline (0.847 here vs Colibri's 0.716 on a real model) and the achievable gains in unknown directions. (d) λ* = 0.3 is a function of loss-scale and entropy at this size; the quality/predictability frontier will shift with scale. (e) Expert miss cost at 19MB int4 vs 1.6MB bf16 differs 12×.
Fix: run Tier A (16 experts) as planned and report the Δ(base→joint) as a function of expert count — even two points (8, 16) test whether the advantage grows or shrinks with E; add a mid-training-checkpoint eval to probe the undertraining confound; and temper all production claims to "direction at toy scale" until Tier C (OLMoE fine-tune, the only scale-anchored tier) is run.

**10. Minor: reporting conflations across experiments — SEVERITY: LOW.**
Exp 1's control row (0.849) and Exp 2's control row (0.847) come from different posthoc runs evaluated at different stream offsets; Exp 1 baseline lm (5.942) and Exp 2 baseline lm (6.285) are presented in adjacent tables without flagging that they are the same model on different eval chunks — a reader could mistake chunk variance for an experimental difference.
Fix: single fixed eval set (attack 6) makes this moot.

### Residual risks if all fixes are applied
Even with a clean backbone-predictability ablation (attack 2), the deployability claim still requires that a *cheap* predictor (Colibri can't co-train) recovers the co-trained predictor's accuracy on the joint backbone — the posthoc-on-joint-backbone test is necessary but the accuracy bar it must meet is the co-trained number, which may itself depend on 6000 steps of joint history. And the entire economic case still hinges on the unbuilt cache/pread benchmark (attack 8).
